## Feature Glossary

### Overview & Fundamentals

**Company overview / key stats** — A snapshot page showing basic information about a public company: market capitalization, share price, 52-week high/low, P/E ratio, EPS, dividend yield, average volume, sector/industry classification, and a brief business description. This is the "front page" when you look up any ticker.

**Income statement** — The financial statement showing a company's revenues, costs, and profits over a period (quarterly or annually). Key line items include revenue, cost of goods sold, gross profit, operating expenses, operating income (EBIT), interest expense, tax expense, and net income. Platforms vary in how many years of history they provide (snapshot vs. 5yr vs. 10yr+).

**Balance sheet** — The financial statement showing what a company owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and shareholders' residual claim (equity) at a point in time. Key line items include cash, accounts receivable, inventory, property/equipment, total debt, accounts payable, and shareholders' equity.

**Cash flow statement** — The financial statement showing actual cash moving in and out of a company, broken into three sections: operating activities (cash from the business), investing activities (capex, acquisitions), and financing activities (debt, dividends, buybacks). Free cash flow (operating cash flow minus capex) is often the most important metric for valuation.

**Revenue by segment** — A breakdown of a company's total revenue by business unit or product line. For example, Apple reports revenue for iPhone, Mac, iPad, Services, and Wearables separately. This reveals which parts of the business are growing or declining and where margins differ. Only Bloomberg, Koyfin, and StockAnalysis offer this — most platforms only show the consolidated total.

**Revenue by geography** — A breakdown of revenue by region or country (e.g., North America, Europe, Asia-Pacific). This reveals geographic concentration risk and exposure to different economic cycles, currencies, and regulatory environments.

**Analyst consensus & targets** — Aggregated Wall Street analyst recommendations (buy, hold, sell counts) and price targets (mean, median, high, low) for a given stock. The consensus is typically based on 10–30 sell-side analysts covering the stock. Most platforms source this from Refinitiv, FactSet, or S&P Capital IQ.

**Earnings estimates & revisions** — Forward-looking EPS and revenue estimates from Wall Street analysts for upcoming quarters and fiscal years. Revisions track how those estimates have changed over time (trending up = bullish, trending down = bearish). Seeking Alpha's "Revisions" factor grade is based entirely on this — stocks with upward-revising estimates tend to outperform.

**Earnings call transcripts** — Full text transcripts of the quarterly conference calls where company management discusses results and answers analyst questions. These contain forward-looking guidance, tone shifts, and qualitative commentary that doesn't appear in financial statements. Only Bloomberg and Seeking Alpha offer these.

**Peer comparison table** — Side-by-side comparison of key financial metrics (P/E, EV/EBITDA, ROE, revenue growth, margins, etc.) for a stock versus its sector peers. Bloomberg's COMP screen is the gold standard — you pick a company, it auto-selects peers, and you can compare across dozens of metrics. This helps determine if a stock is cheap or expensive relative to similar companies.

**Dividend analysis** — Detailed data on a company's dividend: current yield, payout ratio (dividends as % of earnings), dividend growth rate (1yr, 5yr, 10yr CAGR), ex-dividend dates, payment history, and a safety/sustainability assessment. Seeking Alpha's dividend grades are considered best-in-class, scoring safety, growth, yield, and consistency.

**Insider trading data** — Records of stock purchases and sales by company officers, directors, and 10%+ shareholders, sourced from SEC Form 4 filings. Insider buying is generally considered a bullish signal (insiders put their own money in), while insider selling is more ambiguous (may be for personal reasons like diversification or tax planning). Available from nearly every platform except TradingView and TrendSpider.

**Institutional ownership / 13F** — Data from SEC Form 13F filings, which require institutional investment managers with $100M+ in assets to disclose their equity holdings quarterly. This shows which hedge funds, mutual funds, and pension funds own a stock, how many shares they hold, and whether they've been buying or selling. AlphaBreak tracks 20 major funds with an 8.4M-row archive.

**Supply chain mapping** — A visualization of a company's key customers and suppliers, showing revenue exposure and dependency relationships. Bloomberg's SPLC (Supply Chain) screen is the only platform that does this comprehensively. It answers questions like "What % of Company X's revenue comes from Apple?" or "Who are the critical suppliers that could disrupt production?" This is extremely valuable for event-driven analysis (e.g., if a supplier has a factory fire).

**3-statement financials with trends** — All three financial statements (income, balance sheet, cash flow) displayed with historical time-series charts showing how key metrics trend over multiple years. Goes beyond raw tables by letting you visually see revenue growth trajectories, margin expansion/compression, and debt trends at a glance.

**DuPont decomposition** — A framework that breaks Return on Equity (ROE) into three components: profit margin × asset turnover × equity multiplier (leverage). This reveals *why* a company has high or low ROE — is it because they're highly profitable, because they use assets efficiently, or because they're heavily leveraged? Only Bloomberg offers this; it's a standard tool in fundamental analysis taught in every finance program.

**DCF / intrinsic value model** — Discounted Cash Flow analysis that estimates a stock's intrinsic value by projecting future free cash flows and discounting them back to present value. Simply Wall St is the only competitor that builds this into every stock page with adjustable assumptions (growth rate, discount rate, terminal value). Bloomberg has it as a professional tool. This is the foundational valuation method used by institutional investors.

### Charting & Technical Analysis

**Interactive charting** — A dynamic, zoomable price chart where users can change timeframes (1min to monthly), add indicators, draw annotations, and overlay comparisons. The baseline for any trading platform. Quality varies dramatically — TradingView is best-in-class, while Finviz and Koyfin offer basic but functional charts.

**Technical indicators (RSI, MACD, etc.)** — Mathematical calculations applied to price/volume data to identify trends, momentum, volatility, and potential reversals. Common indicators include RSI (Relative Strength Index — overbought/oversold), MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence — trend/momentum), Bollinger Bands (volatility), moving averages (SMA, EMA — trend direction), and Stochastic (momentum). thinkorswim offers 400+ built-in studies, the most of any platform.

**Drawing tools (Fib, trendlines)** — Manual annotation tools for charts: trendlines (connecting highs or lows), Fibonacci retracements (key support/resistance levels based on the 61.8%/38.2% ratios), channels, rectangles, Gann fans, pitchforks, and Elliott Wave labels. These help traders visually identify chart patterns and key price levels. TradingView has the most comprehensive drawing toolkit.

**Multi-timeframe analysis** — Viewing or overlaying indicators from multiple timeframes simultaneously on a single chart (e.g., seeing the daily RSI and hourly RSI together, or checking if a trade setup aligns across weekly, daily, and 15-minute charts). TrendSpider is the only platform that does this natively in a single chart view — most others require you to open separate chart windows.

**Auto-detected trendlines** — Algorithmic identification and drawing of trendlines, support/resistance levels, and chart patterns without manual input. The software scans price history and automatically plots the most significant lines. TrendSpider is the only platform that offers this as a core feature — every other platform requires manual drawing.

**Auto Fibonacci levels** — Algorithmic placement of Fibonacci retracement and extension levels on a chart. The software identifies significant swing highs and lows and automatically calculates the key Fib levels (23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, 78.6%). Only TrendSpider does this; all other platforms require manual placement.

**Candlestick pattern recognition** — Automatic detection and labeling of candlestick patterns on charts (e.g., doji, hammer, engulfing, morning star, evening star, three white soldiers). These patterns are used in technical analysis to predict short-term price reversals or continuations. TrendSpider and Finviz both auto-detect these patterns.

**Seasonality patterns (return heatmap)** — Historical analysis of how a stock typically performs in each month or week of the year, displayed as a color-coded heatmap. For example, showing that AAPL has historically returned +3.2% in January and -1.1% in September over the past 20 years. Useful for timing entries/exits around recurring seasonal trends.

**Custom scripting language** — A programming language built into the platform for creating custom indicators, strategies, and alerts. Bloomberg has BQL (Bloomberg Query Language), TradingView has Pine Script (most popular — huge community library), and thinkorswim has ThinkScript. These allow power users to build analysis tools that don't exist natively.

**Raindrop / volume profile charts** — A proprietary chart type (TrendSpider) that visualizes volume distribution within each candle, showing where buying and selling pressure concentrated at different price levels. Related to traditional Volume Profile (TPO) charts that show a histogram of volume traded at each price level, identifying high-volume nodes (support/resistance) and low-volume gaps (potential fast-move zones).

**Technical opinion signal** — An automated buy/sell/hold recommendation based purely on technical indicators. TradingView shows a widget summarizing oscillators and moving averages (e.g., "Strong Buy — 9 of 11 indicators bullish"). Barchart has its proprietary "Barchart Technical Opinion" composite signal. AlphaBreak's trend break detection serves a similar purpose.

### Options Analytics

**Options chain** — A table displaying all available options contracts for a given security, organized by expiration date and strike price, showing bid/ask prices, volume, open interest, and implied volatility for both calls and puts. This is the starting point for any options analysis or trade.

**Greeks (Δ, Γ, Θ, V)** — Sensitivity measures for options pricing. **Delta (Δ)**: how much the option price moves per $1 move in the underlying. **Gamma (Γ)**: rate of change of delta (acceleration). **Theta (Θ)**: time decay — how much value the option loses per day. **Vega (V)**: sensitivity to changes in implied volatility. thinkorswim displays these most comprehensively with real-time updates.

**Fair value (Black-Scholes, Binomial)** — Mathematical models that calculate the theoretical price of an options contract based on the underlying price, strike, time to expiration, volatility, risk-free rate, and dividends. Black-Scholes is the classic closed-form solution for European options; Binomial Tree is a discrete-time model that handles American options and early exercise. Comparing fair value to market price reveals whether an option is overpriced or underpriced.

**IV history / vol surface** — Historical charts of implied volatility over time (showing how expensive options are relative to their history) and the volatility surface — a 3D visualization of IV across different strike prices and expirations simultaneously. The surface reveals skew (puts being more expensive than calls) and term structure (near-term vs. long-term IV). Essential for options sellers and volatility traders.

**Unusual options activity** — Detection of options contracts trading at significantly higher volume than their average (typically 2x+ the 5-day or 20-day average). Large, unusual orders may indicate informed trading — someone with an edge betting on a directional move. thinkorswim's "Sizzle Index" and Barchart's screeners are the best-known implementations. Often shows block trades (large single orders) and sweep orders (urgent fills across multiple exchanges).

**Probability of profit** — A calculation showing the statistical likelihood that an options position will be profitable at expiration, based on implied volatility and the current price distribution. For example, a short put at the 30-delta strike has roughly a 70% probability of profit. thinkorswim is the only free platform that shows this natively in the options chain and trade builder.

**Risk profile / P&L diagram** — A visual graph showing the profit and loss of an options position across all possible underlying prices at expiration (and often before expiration using theoretical pricing). This lets you see max profit, max loss, breakeven points, and how the position behaves at different prices. Essential for multi-leg strategies. Only Bloomberg and thinkorswim offer this.

**Strategy builder (spreads, condors)** — A tool for constructing multi-leg options strategies by selecting individual contracts and combining them. Includes templates for common strategies: vertical spreads (bull call, bear put), iron condors, butterflies, straddles, strangles, calendars, and diagonals. thinkorswim's strategy builder is considered the industry best, with point-and-click construction and instant P&L visualization.

**Market Maker Move** — The expected price move implied by options pricing around a binary event (usually earnings). Calculated from the at-the-money straddle price — if the ATM straddle costs $5 on a $100 stock, the market implies a ±5% move. Traders use this to decide whether to buy or sell volatility into earnings. Only Bloomberg and thinkorswim display this prominently.

**IV crush modeling** — Prediction of the implied volatility decline that occurs after an earnings announcement or other binary event. Options prices embed uncertainty about the event; once the event passes, that uncertainty premium collapses ("crush"). Modeling this helps options traders estimate how much value their position will lose from IV contraction even if the stock moves in their favor.

**ORATS historical data** — Data from ORATS (Options Research & Technology Services), a specialized provider of historical options data including full IV history, earnings IV spikes and post-earnings behavior, historical Greeks, and options volume/open interest going back years. No competitor currently integrates ORATS data — it's a paid data feed ($100/mo+) that provides institutional-grade options analytics.

**Options flow dashboard** — An aggregated, real-time view of unusual options activity across the entire market (not just one ticker). Shows large block trades, sweep orders, and whale-sized positions as they happen. Includes filtering by ticker, strategy type, premium size, and sentiment (bullish vs. bearish). Think of it as a radar screen for what "smart money" is doing in the options market.

### AI, Scoring & Quantitative

**Quant letter grades (A-F)** — A system (pioneered by Seeking Alpha) that scores every stock on five factors and assigns A+ through F grades: **Value** (P/E, P/S, P/B, EV/EBITDA vs. sector), **Growth** (revenue, EPS, EBITDA growth rates), **Profitability** (margins, ROE, ROA), **Momentum** (price performance over 3, 6, 12 months vs. sector), and **EPS Revisions** (direction and magnitude of analyst estimate changes). Each grade compares the stock to its sector median. Simply Wall St's "Snowflake" diagram is a visual variant with different factors.

**AI trade scoring** — Machine learning-powered grading of individual trades on entry quality, exit quality, and timing. Evaluates whether you bought near support, sold near resistance, caught the trend direction, and managed risk appropriately. No competitor offers this — it's unique to AlphaBreak's trade journal.

**Composite technical + fundamental score** — A single unified score that combines technical analysis signals (trend direction, momentum, support/resistance) with fundamental data (valuation, growth, quality) and optionally options flow data. No competitor synthesizes across all three dimensions — they're all siloed (Seeking Alpha = fundamentals only, TrendSpider = technicals only, thinkorswim = options only).

**Smart checklists (multi-indicator)** — Configurable rule sets that check multiple conditions simultaneously and show which are met. For example: "RSI < 30 AND price above 200 SMA AND bullish 13F accumulation AND insider buying AND positive earnings revision." TrendSpider offers automated condition monitoring; thinkorswim allows custom scans but without the checklist UI.

**Regime-aware analysis** — Analysis that automatically adjusts its interpretation based on the current market regime (bull market, bear market, high-volatility, low-volatility, ranging/choppy). The same RSI reading of 30 means something different in a trending bear market vs. a ranging market. No competitor adjusts their signals or scoring based on regime — they all treat every market condition identically.

**News NLP sentiment scoring** — Natural language processing applied to news headlines and articles to generate a quantitative sentiment score (bullish/bearish/neutral). Advanced implementations use financial-domain models like FinBERT (trained specifically on financial text) rather than generic sentiment classifiers. No competitor offers real-time NLP-scored news feeds for individual tickers.

**Earnings surprise prediction (ML)** — A machine learning model that predicts whether a company will beat or miss earnings estimates, using features like options IV behavior, analyst revision patterns, historical surprise tendencies, and sector trends. No competitor offers this — most just show historical surprise data without predictive modeling.

**Signal accuracy tracking** — A transparency feature that records the platform's own signals/recommendations and publicly tracks how they performed. For example: "Our trend break signal fired bullish on AAPL on March 15 — the stock rose 4.2% in the following 5 days." Seeking Alpha publishes some backtested performance for their Quant Ratings, but no platform provides real-time accuracy dashboards for live signals.

**Short interest / squeeze score** — Data on the percentage of a stock's float that has been sold short (short float %), the days-to-cover ratio (short shares ÷ average daily volume — how many days it would take all shorts to cover), and a composite "squeeze risk" score indicating the probability of a short squeeze (where short sellers are forced to buy back, driving the price up rapidly). Finviz is the primary free source for this data.

### Data & Infrastructure

**Real-time data** — Live, streaming price quotes with no delay. Standard market data has a 15-minute delay for free users on most platforms. Real-time data requires exchange licensing fees (NYSE, NASDAQ, etc.), which is why it's typically a paid feature. Polygon.io ($199/mo) is a common provider for startups.

**Delayed data (free)** — Price data with a 15-minute delay from the live market, offered at no cost. Sufficient for end-of-day analysis, swing trading, and position management, but inadequate for day trading or real-time decision-making.

**API access** — Programmatic access to the platform's data and features via REST API or similar interfaces. Allows quant traders, developers, and fintech companies to build custom tools, automate workflows, and integrate data into their own systems. Bloomberg's BQL is the gold standard; most platforms don't offer API access at all.

**WebSocket streaming** — A persistent, bidirectional connection that pushes real-time data to the client as it happens (vs. REST polling where the client repeatedly asks "any updates?"). Used for live price feeds, real-time trade signals, and portfolio updates. Requires server-side infrastructure (Redis Pub/Sub, Socket.IO, or similar).

**Mobile app** — A native iOS/Android application providing on-the-go access to the platform. TradingView and thinkorswim have the best mobile experiences. AlphaBreak plans a React Native MVP for Q3 2026.

**Dark pool data** — Trading volume data from dark pools (private exchanges where institutional investors trade large blocks of shares without displaying orders publicly). Dark pool activity can signal institutional accumulation or distribution before it appears in public market data. AlphaBreak has 621K rows of weekly dark pool volume across 22,940 symbols — very few competitors offer this data.

**Forex correlations** — Analysis of how currency pairs move relative to each other and to equities. Includes correlation matrices, USD strength indexing, and cross-pair relationship analysis. AlphaBreak covers 21 currency pairs with 54 years of historical data (123K rows). Useful for understanding macro risk exposure and hedging currency-sensitive positions.
