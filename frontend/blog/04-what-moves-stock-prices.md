# The Only Three Things That Actually Move Stock Prices

*Reading time: 13 minutes*

---

## The Simplification That Actually Helps

Trading media produces an endless stream of explanations for why prices move. Earnings reports, Fed decisions, geopolitical events, analyst upgrades, insider activity, technical breakouts, options expiration, sector rotation, moon phases (yes, really) — the list is infinite and exhausting.

Most of this is noise piled on top of three simple forces. Once you see the underlying structure, the apparent chaos becomes comprehensible.

**The three forces that move every stock price are:**

1. **Supply and demand imbalance** (order flow)
2. **New information** (events)
3. **Sentiment shift** (collective emotional state)

Everything else — indicators, patterns, news headlines, analyst targets — is either a derivative of these three forces or a measurement of them. There are no other mechanisms.

This isn't a simplification that sacrifices accuracy. It's a simplification that reveals the accurate structure underneath the noise.

---

## Force 1: Supply and Demand

### The Tautological Truth

At its most basic level, price movement is defined by supply and demand. If more buyers want to purchase than sellers want to sell at the current price, the price rises until it attracts enough sellers. If more sellers want to unload than buyers want to take, the price falls until it attracts enough buyers.

This is true by definition. It's how every auction-based market works — from stock exchanges to eBay to fish markets.

What makes it interesting is not the definition, but the question: **what creates supply and demand imbalance?**

### Structural Sources of Supply/Demand

These are the mechanisms, mostly invisible to retail traders, that produce price pressure:

**Index fund flows**: When SPY (S&P 500 ETF) receives $500 million in new inflows, its fund manager must buy the underlying stocks in proportion. Stocks with higher weight in the index (AAPL, MSFT, NVDA) get more demand. This is mechanical buying, independent of any fundamental view on the companies.

**Index inclusion/exclusion**: When a stock is added to a major index (S&P 500, Russell 2000, Nasdaq 100), index funds tracking that index must buy it. This creates days or weeks of concentrated demand, often pushing the price up 5-15% around the inclusion date.

**Institutional rebalancing**: Many large funds rebalance quarterly or monthly to maintain target allocations. If tech stocks have outperformed, a 60/40 portfolio now has more tech exposure than intended. Rebalancing means selling some tech — supply created by mandate, not by opinion.

**Options market maker hedging**: When options trading creates large imbalances in market makers' positions, those market makers must buy or sell the underlying stock to hedge their exposure. This can create predictable buying or selling pressure, especially near options expiration.

**Margin calls and forced liquidation**: During sharp declines, leveraged positions get margin calls. Traders must sell (or are sold out automatically) regardless of their view on the stock. This creates additional supply that accelerates declines.

### The Implication for Traders

Most retail traders think of price as "fair value" — a reflection of what the stock is worth. This is largely wrong.

Price is the current equilibrium between buyer urgency and seller urgency. Urgency is shaped by mandate, leverage, fund flows, and mechanical rules — not by whether participants think the company is "undervalued."

A stock can remain cheap (relative to fundamentals) for years because there's no urgency among buyers. A stock can rally despite weak fundamentals because mechanical demand (index inclusion, fund inflows) overwhelms opinion.

**The trader's question is not "what is this stock worth?" but "where is urgency accumulating?"**

## Force 2: New Information

### The Efficient Market Hypothesis (Honest Version)

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) claims that prices reflect all available information. In its strongest form, this means no analysis can produce excess returns because anything knowable is already priced in.

The strong form is demonstrably false. If it were true, no one would profit from trading, and markets would exhibit no patterns. Both claims fail empirically.

But the weaker forms of EMH are approximately correct most of the time: prices adjust quickly to widely-known public information, and the window for profiting from public information is usually measured in minutes or hours, not days.

**This is why news-based trading is so difficult for retail**: by the time you see the headline on your phone, the institutional traders have already moved the price. You're trading against people who saw the information milliseconds faster — or algorithms that parsed it faster than human reading speed.

### Where Information Still Creates Opportunity

The EMH assumes perfect information processing. In reality, information processing is imperfect in specific, identifiable ways:

**Interpretation lag**: Markets react quickly to the headline but slowly to the full implications. A company reports earnings in line with expectations but guides down for the next quarter. The stock initially moves on the headline ("in line") but then drifts down over days as analysts digest the guidance.

**Information diffusion**: Some participants learn about events before others. Not through illegal insider trading, but through better networks, faster data feeds, and more sophisticated analysis. The price move happens in waves as information spreads from fastest to slowest participants.

**Overreaction**: Markets sometimes move further than the information justifies, driven by panic or enthusiasm. The correction back toward "fair" value is a tradeable opportunity.

**Underreaction**: Markets sometimes don't fully price in significant information for days or weeks, especially if the information requires analysis (footnotes in an SEC filing, implications of an FDA decision). Early readers benefit from the delayed response.

### Types of Information Events

Not all information affects prices equally. Roughly in order of impact:

1. **Earnings reports** (quarterly) — The single largest scheduled catalyst for most stocks
2. **Macro data releases** (Fed decisions, CPI, jobs reports) — Affect broad markets and sector rotation
3. **Regulatory events** (FDA decisions, SEC actions, court rulings) — Binary outcomes that can move stocks 20-50% in a day
4. **Analyst actions** (upgrades, downgrades, price target changes) — Modest impact, usually 2-5%
5. **News headlines** (product launches, management changes, partnerships) — Variable impact depending on novelty and interpretation

The calendar of information events is mostly public. Earnings dates are announced weeks in advance. Fed meetings are scheduled months out. Knowing what events are coming in the next 30 days is a basic competence — but one most retail traders don't maintain.

## Force 3: Sentiment

### What Sentiment Actually Is

Sentiment is the hardest force to define precisely but the most important to understand. It is not:

- A fact about the stock
- A fact about the company
- A rational analysis

It is a fact about the current emotional and expectational state of market participants collectively.

**Sentiment answers the question: what does the average participant feel about the future right now?** Are they optimistic or fearful? Confident or uncertain? Exuberant or exhausted?

### Why Sentiment Matters

In theory, rational analysis should drive price. In practice, many participants are not rational — they are responding to emotion, social pressure, recent performance, and story narratives.

When sentiment is euphoric, participants pay more for future earnings than they would in a neutral state. When sentiment is depressed, they pay less for the same earnings. The company hasn't changed. The price has.

This is not a market failure. It's a feature of markets populated by humans. And it's why sentiment is a real, measurable force that moves prices — separate from fundamentals and independent of new information.

### Measuring Sentiment

Sentiment is invisible by nature, but it leaves visible traces:

**VIX (Volatility Index)**: Often called the "fear gauge." It measures the implied volatility of S&P 500 options — how much the market expects prices to move in the next 30 days. High VIX = high uncertainty = fear-driven sentiment. Low VIX = calm markets = complacent sentiment.

**Put/Call ratio**: The volume of put options (bearish bets) relative to call options (bullish bets). Extreme ratios in either direction often mark sentiment extremes.

**Margin debt**: The total amount borrowed to buy stocks. High margin debt indicates confident (or overconfident) sentiment. Low margin debt indicates cautious sentiment.

**Survey data**: AAII (American Association of Individual Investors) surveys track whether individual investors feel bullish, bearish, or neutral. Extreme readings are contrarian indicators — when retail is maximally bullish, a top is often near.

**Financial media tone**: Not a precise measure, but when magazine covers are screaming "New Era!" or "The End of Stocks!", sentiment is at an extreme.

### Asymmetry: Fear Is Faster Than Greed

One of the most reliable observations about sentiment: **markets go up gradually and go down suddenly.**

This isn't an accident. Fear and greed are not symmetric emotions. Greed builds slowly — people need to see rising prices before they become enthusiastic, and that process takes weeks or months. Fear spikes quickly — a 5% drop can trigger panic selling within hours.

The asymmetry shows up in price action. Bull markets grind upward for months. Bear markets crash downward in weeks. The same information (a 10% move) feels different depending on direction because fear is louder than greed.

**The trader's implication**: sentiment-driven moves are worth paying attention to, but especially so on the downside. Panic creates opportunities because panic overshoots.

## How the Three Forces Interact

The three forces don't operate in isolation. They form a feedback system:

```
Information → Sentiment → Supply/Demand → Price movement
       ↑                                        ↓
       └────────── Price movement ──────────────┘
```

**The normal causal chain:**

1. New information arrives (earnings beat)
2. Sentiment shifts (optimism increases)
3. Supply/demand imbalance appears (more buyers than sellers)
4. Price moves (up)

**The feedback loop:**

5. Rising price itself affects sentiment (momentum traders become interested)
6. Changed sentiment creates more supply/demand imbalance
7. Price moves further
8. Eventually the movement exhausts itself as sentiment reaches an extreme or new information arrives

This feedback is why price movements often overshoot what the initial information justified. Information triggers sentiment, sentiment creates imbalance, imbalance creates price movement, price movement reinforces sentiment, and so on.

### The Informationless Move

Sometimes prices move without any identifiable new information. A stock drops 3% on a quiet Tuesday with no news.

These moves are usually driven by either:
- **Pure supply/demand**: Mechanical flows (index rebalancing, options hedging, fund redemptions)
- **Pure sentiment shift**: Collective mood change without triggering event

Informationless moves are common. They're why trying to explain every price movement with news is a losing battle — sometimes there isn't a news story. There's just order flow.

### The Narrative Trap

When prices move, financial media rushes to explain why. These explanations are usually narratives constructed after the fact to match the price action.

"Stocks fell today on concerns about inflation."
"Stocks rose today as investors looked past inflation concerns."

These are the same inflation data, interpreted opposite ways. The narrative is manufactured to match whichever direction the market happened to move. The narrative is not the cause of the move — it's the story told afterward.

**Professional traders largely ignore these narratives.** They recognize that most explanations are retrospective rationalization. The real question is what order flow looks like, not what CNBC said it was about.

## Practical Application

### 1. Stop Looking for "The Reason"

When a stock moves unexpectedly, resist the urge to find a headline that explains it. Sometimes there is no reason. The move might be driven by a fund reallocation, options market-maker hedging, or sentiment shifting. None of these are visible in news.

Instead, ask: does this move change my thesis? If your thesis was based on fundamentals and they haven't changed, a 3% move might be noise. If your thesis was based on technical structure and the move broke that structure, the thesis has been invalidated.

### 2. Trade With the Regime of Sentiment

In euphoric markets, buying breakouts often works because participants are eager to chase strength. In fearful markets, buying panic often works because selling has overshot. In neutral sentiment, mean reversion strategies tend to outperform.

Match your strategy to the sentiment regime. VIX readings are a rough proxy — under 15 suggests complacency, over 25 suggests fear.

### 3. Know the Calendar

Every Monday, check the information calendar for the week:

- Earnings releases for stocks you hold or watch
- Macro data releases (Fed meetings, CPI, jobs)
- Sector-specific events (FDA decisions, oil inventories, retail sales)

Informed traders trade around scheduled events with awareness. Uninformed traders get blindsided by them.

### 4. Watch Order Flow, Not Just Price

Price is the result. Order flow is the cause. The tools for reading order flow include:

- Volume on significant moves (high volume = broad participation)
- Dark pool activity (institutional positioning)
- Unusual options volume (informed positioning ahead of events)
- Institutional holdings changes (13F filings, quarterly)

You don't need expensive tools. Much of this data is public. What you need is the habit of looking at it.

## The Hierarchy of Causes

When you analyze a price move, move through this hierarchy:

1. **Is there scheduled information that explains this?** (Earnings, Fed, economic data)
2. **Is there unscheduled information?** (Breaking news, company-specific events)
3. **Is there a sentiment regime shift?** (VIX moving, broader market panic or euphoria)
4. **Is there mechanical order flow?** (Index rebalancing, options expiration, fund flows)
5. **Is this pure noise?** (Small move, no identifiable cause — often the right answer)

If you go through this list and can't identify a cause, the most likely explanation is that the move is within normal daily variance and doesn't require explanation. Not every movement is meaningful.

---

## The Simplification That Frees You

Once you internalize that only three forces move prices, trading becomes less overwhelming. You don't need to track every news story, every analyst note, every technical indicator. You need to track:

- Supply/demand imbalances (volume, order flow, institutional positioning)
- Information events (earnings calendar, economic calendar)
- Sentiment state (VIX, survey data, market mood)

These three categories compress the infinite noise of financial media into a manageable analytical framework. Everything else is a measurement of one of these three or a derivative of them.

The market isn't chaotic. It's complicated. But the complication is built from simple building blocks. Once you see the blocks, the structure becomes visible.
