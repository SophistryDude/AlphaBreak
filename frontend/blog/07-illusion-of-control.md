# The Illusion of Control: Why "Feeling It" Is Usually Wrong

*Reading time: 13 minutes*

---

## The Experiment That Explains Why Traders Fail

In 1975, psychologist Ellen Langer ran a series of experiments that revealed something uncomfortable about human cognition. She had participants bet on lottery tickets under two conditions:

- **Condition A**: Tickets were randomly assigned to participants
- **Condition B**: Participants chose their own ticket numbers

When the researcher later offered to buy back the tickets, participants in Condition B demanded about four times more money than those in Condition A — even though both conditions had identical odds of winning.

Why? Because choosing their own numbers created the feeling of control, even in a game that was pure chance. The illusion was so strong that participants placed real financial value on it.

This is the illusion of control, and it's one of the most documented cognitive biases in psychology. Humans systematically overestimate their ability to influence random or semi-random outcomes. And nowhere is this bias more financially destructive than in trading.

---

## Why This Matters for Traders

Trading is an activity with high variance, delayed feedback, and a lot of participant skill. It sits in an uncomfortable middle zone — it's not pure luck (skill matters) and it's not pure skill (luck matters enormously). This creates the perfect environment for the illusion of control to take root.

After a winning trade, your brain says: "I chose this trade. It won. Therefore my judgment is good."

After a losing trade, your brain says: "I chose this trade. It lost. But that was unusual — normally my judgment is good."

Both conclusions are likely unjustified based on the sample size. But both feel real. And they drive subsequent behavior.

---

## The Fundamental Problem: Distinguishing Skill from Luck

Here's the hardest concept in trading: **in any given small sample of trades, you cannot reliably tell whether you're skilled or lucky.**

This is not a matter of effort or intelligence. It's a mathematical fact about variance.

### The Sample Size Requirement

Consider a trader with a 55% win rate (which would be decent for many strategies). After 10 trades:
- Expected wins: 5.5
- Standard deviation: ~1.6
- A 3-7 win range is statistically unremarkable

After 10 trades, the same trader could have 3 wins (looks terrible) or 7 wins (looks great) due to pure variance. Neither outcome tells you much about their actual skill.

To distinguish a 55% win rate from a 50% win rate with reasonable confidence, you need approximately 300-500 trades. With a smaller sample, the results are dominated by noise.

**The practical implication**: after 10 trades, you know almost nothing about your skill. After 50, you have a weak signal. After 200, you have meaningful data. After 500, you can start making confident statements about your edge.

### What This Means for Your Recent Results

If you've been trading for 6 months and have 60 trades, your results tell you:
- Did you follow your rules? (Yes/no — this is observable)
- Are you approximately profitable or approximately unprofitable? (Rough signal)

They don't reliably tell you:
- What your true win rate is (noise too large)
- Whether your last 10 trades represent your "current form"
- Whether a streak was skill or luck

The uncomfortable truth: after 60 trades, you might be a 45% win rate trader who got lucky to be positive, or a 58% win rate trader who got unlucky to be negative. The difference matters enormously, but you can't tell from the data yet.

## The Four Biases That Reinforce the Illusion

The illusion of control doesn't exist in isolation. It's reinforced by four other cognitive biases that together create a potent mix of overconfidence.

### 1. Confirmation Bias

**What it is**: The tendency to seek and remember information that confirms your existing beliefs while filtering out contradictory information.

**In trading**: After entering a long position, you read bullish analyst notes carefully and skim past bearish ones. When price dips, you see "buying opportunity." When it rallies, you see "confirmation of thesis." Your brain actively reorganizes incoming information around your position.

**The result**: you think the evidence supports your trade more strongly than it actually does. You become overconfident, take larger positions, and miss warning signs.

**The antidote**: Actively seek disconfirming evidence before each trade. Ask: "What would make me wrong?" and genuinely look for answers. If you can't find any, your thesis is probably under-examined, not unusually strong.

### 2. Recency Bias

**What it is**: Overweighting recent events compared to the full historical record.

**In trading**: Your last 3 trades shape your emotional state far more than your last 30. Three wins = you feel invincible. Three losses = you feel broken. Neither feeling is justified by statistically meaningful information.

**The mechanism**: recent events are cognitively "closer" — easier to recall, more emotionally vivid, more mentally salient. Your brain processes them as if they were more important than older events, even when they're not.

**The result**: you abandon working systems after short losing streaks and double down on luck-based winning streaks.

**The antidote**: judge your performance on rolling 50+ trade samples, not on individual trades or short streaks. The signal emerges at scale; noise dominates at small samples.

### 3. Anchoring

**What it is**: Fixating on an initial value (an "anchor") that influences subsequent judgments, even when the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant.

**In trading**: the most common anchor is your entry price. You bought AAPL at $190. Suddenly that price is "special" — not because the market cares, but because your brain treats it as the reference point for all subsequent decisions.

**The result**: you exit winners the moment they return to breakeven (because the anchor says "this is where I started, losing from here would hurt") rather than letting them run to your target. You hold losers past your stop because "it just needs to get back to $190."

**The antidote**: recognize that entry price is an arbitrary historical event that doesn't affect future probabilities. The market does not care what you paid. Decisions should be based on current price action and forward probabilities, not on your reference point.

### 4. Narrative Bias

**What it is**: Constructing coherent stories to explain random or semi-random events after the fact.

**In trading**: after a winning trade, you construct a story: "I saw the pattern, analyzed the setup, and executed perfectly." After a losing trade, you construct a different story: "The market was irrational," or "I should have seen the warning signs."

**The problem**: both stories make you feel better but neither is necessarily true. Your winning trade might have been a lucky correlation with an unrelated move. Your losing trade might have been a well-reasoned bet that didn't pan out due to variance.

**The result**: you draw false lessons from random events. "Winning trades" teach you to repeat whatever you think you did right. "Losing trades" teach you to avoid whatever you think you did wrong. Both lessons may be based on noise, not signal.

**The antidote**: when reviewing trades, resist the urge to construct explanations. Just record the facts: what was the setup, what did you do, what happened. Over 50+ trades, patterns emerge from the data without needing retroactive narratives.

---

## The Compound Effect

These four biases plus the illusion of control don't just add up — they multiply. Each one reinforces the others.

You have confirmation bias: you see evidence supporting your trades. Then recency bias: you remember your recent wins. Then narrative bias: you construct a story about being in the zone. Then overconfidence: you increase position sizes. Then a big loss from the oversized position: anchoring kicks in, holding past the stop hoping to return to breakeven. The loss compounds.

Each bias alone would be manageable. Together, they can destroy an account in weeks.

This is why professional traders spend so much time on process and so little time on predictions. The process exists to counteract the cognitive biases. The prediction doesn't matter nearly as much as the execution.

## The Skilled Trader's Epistemics

What distinguishes traders who actually become skilled from those who remain perpetually fooled by randomness?

### They Maintain Uncertainty

Skilled traders have a precise sense of what they know versus what they're guessing. They distinguish:

- Facts (price is at $190, volume is 2x average)
- Probabilities (the setup has historically worked about 55% of the time)
- Speculation (this time it feels especially strong)

They never collapse these categories. A speculation is not promoted to a probability just because it's a strong feeling. A probability is not promoted to certainty just because it's worked recently.

### They Separate Decision Quality from Outcome

A good decision is one that was appropriate given the information available. A good outcome is one that happened to work out. These are different things.

In a high-variance activity, good decisions sometimes produce bad outcomes (you took a good trade that happened to lose). Bad decisions sometimes produce good outcomes (you took an impulsive trade that happened to win).

Skilled traders evaluate their decisions, not their outcomes. This allows them to continue making good decisions even during losing streaks, and to avoid being reinforced for lucky wins that came from bad decisions.

### They Track Process Metrics

Beyond P&L, skilled traders track:
- Percentage of trades that followed their plan
- Average time spent on pre-trade checklist
- Number of rule violations per month
- Correlation between emotional state and decision quality

These process metrics reveal whether you're executing well, independent of short-term outcomes.

### They Embrace Drawdowns as Information

When a strategy enters a drawdown, most traders ask: "Is the strategy broken?" Skilled traders ask a more precise question: "Are the losses consistent with normal variance, or do they indicate a regime change?"

The tools for answering this include:
- Was the drawdown within expected range based on historical variance?
- Are the losses spread across normal setup types, or clustered in one type?
- Did market regime change (trending to ranging, or vice versa)?

If the drawdown is within normal variance, the strategy isn't broken — you're just experiencing the losing phase that variance guarantees will occur. Abandoning the strategy at this point is the most common way traders turn temporary losses into permanent ones.

## The Systematic Antidotes

Given all of this, here's a concrete set of practices to counteract the illusion of control and related biases:

### 1. Write Your Thesis Before Entering

Before every trade, write down:
- Why you're taking this trade (specific setup criteria met)
- What would invalidate the thesis (stop loss conditions)
- What the target is (profit taking conditions)

Writing creates a record that cannot be revised after the fact. Later, when you review the trade, you can compare what you said to what you did.

### 2. Review on Longer Time Scales

Don't evaluate your trading on individual trades or short streaks. Evaluate on 50+ trade samples.

Every month, calculate:
- Win rate
- Average win vs average loss
- Profit factor
- Maximum drawdown
- Number of rule violations

Compare these metrics to the previous month. Trends become visible over several months; individual months are mostly noise.

### 3. Actively Seek Disconfirming Evidence

Before entering any trade, spend 60 seconds deliberately looking for reasons not to take it:
- What are the bearish arguments against a long trade?
- What is the stock's downside scenario?
- What would a skeptical analyst say?

This isn't pessimism. It's epistemic hygiene. If you still want to take the trade after considering the counterarguments, your conviction is better supported.

### 4. Use Mechanical Execution

Whenever possible, replace in-the-moment decisions with pre-set orders:
- Stop loss orders placed with the broker
- Take profit orders at your target
- Alerts that trigger specific pre-defined actions

Mechanical execution removes the opportunity for biases to influence the decision at the moment of emotional pressure.

### 5. Journal With Structured Fields

Journal entries should not just be prose descriptions. They should include:
- Date and ticker
- Setup type
- Entry price, stop loss, target
- Position size and risk
- Pre-trade emotional state (calm, agitated, excited, fearful)
- Post-trade outcome
- Rules followed (yes/no for each relevant rule)
- Notes

Structured data allows pattern detection that prose doesn't. After 100 entries, you can query: "What's my win rate when I was agitated pre-trade vs. calm?" The answer is often illuminating.

## The Permission to Be Uncertain

There's a strange psychological cost to accepting the illusion of control: it feels like admitting defeat. The feeling that you have skill, that you see patterns others don't, that you're "in tune with the market" — these feelings are enjoyable. Giving them up in exchange for honest uncertainty seems like a downgrade.

But consider the alternative. Traders who believe they have more skill than they can demonstrate will take bigger risks, deviate from rules more often, and blow up more frequently. The illusion of control is expensive.

The traders who survive long-term are the ones who accept a slightly less flattering self-concept in exchange for more accurate decision-making. They don't feel like market geniuses. They feel like rule-followers who happen to profit because the rules are designed to exploit small, consistent edges.

This isn't defeat. It's epistemic adulthood. The market is too complex to "feel." It can only be traded with systems designed to operate despite feelings. The sooner you trade the illusion for the system, the sooner you become genuinely skilled — not in the sense of "I can predict the market," but in the sense of "I can execute a plan that has a positive expected value across hundreds of trades."

The second kind of skill actually produces profits. The first kind just produces stories.
