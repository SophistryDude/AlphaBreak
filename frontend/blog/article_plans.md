### Article 1: Why Most New Traders Lose Money (And How to Not Be One of Them)

**SEO targets**: "why do traders lose money", "common trading mistakes", "new trader mistakes"
**Word count**: ~3,000
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Trade journal + AI scoring catches the behavioral patterns described

**Plan:**

1. **The statistic and why it's misunderstood**
   - 70-90% of retail traders lose money. But this is an aggregate — it doesn't mean each individual has a 70-90% chance of failure.
   - Separate the observation (most lose) from the mechanism (why they lose). These are different epistemic claims.

2. **The three structural reasons traders lose**
   - **Information asymmetry**: Not about insider info. About the difference between having data and having a framework to interpret it. Analogy: having all the ingredients vs. knowing how to cook.
   - **Negative-sum game after costs**: Spread, commissions, slippage. Before any edge, you're starting at -1 to -3% annually. Your edge must first overcome friction.
   - **Behavioral asymmetry**: Humans cut winners early (fear of losing gains) and hold losers (hope of recovery). This is prospect theory — empirically verified, not opinion. Kahneman/Tversky reference, explained accessibly.

3. **The survivorship problem in trading education**
   - You see successful traders on social media. You don't see the 90% who quit. The visible sample is not the representative sample.
   - Analogy: studying only NBA players to learn about basketball success rates.

4. **What survivors actually do differently**
   - They define risk before reward (position sizing, stop losses)
   - They trade with a thesis, not a feeling
   - They review trades systematically (journal)
   - They accept losses as a cost of doing business, not as personal failure

5. **The constraint-based reframe**
   - Instead of "how do I make money trading?" ask "what are the constraints that prevent most people from making money, and how do I operate within those constraints?"
   - The constraints: costs, psychology, information processing, time. Address each deliberately.

6. **Practical starting framework**
   - Paper trade for 30 days (no exceptions)
   - Risk no more than 1% per trade
   - Journal every trade with thesis + outcome
   - Review weekly: what worked, what was luck, what was skill

### Article 2: Risk Management: The One Skill That Separates Survivors from Blow-Ups

**SEO targets**: "trading risk management", "position sizing", "how much to risk per trade"
**Word count**: ~3,500
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Portfolio tracker with AI-scored journal, automated position size suggestions

**Plan:**

1. **Why risk management is the foundation, not the accessory**
   - Most education: find entries → manage risk. Reality: manage risk → find entries within that framework.
   - Analogy: an architect designs the structure (load-bearing capacity) before choosing paint colors. Risk management is the structure; entries are the paint.

2. **The math of ruin (explained without heavy math)**
   - Losing 50% requires gaining 100% to break even. Table showing asymmetry:
     - -10% → need +11.1%
     - -25% → need +33.3%
     - -50% → need +100%
     - -90% → need +900%
   - The relationship is non-linear. Large losses are disproportionately harder to recover from. This is mathematical fact, not opinion.

3. **The 1-2% rule: what it is and why it works**
   - Never risk more than 1-2% of total capital on a single trade
   - Walk through the calculation: $25,000 account → 1% risk = $250 max loss → if stop loss is $5 away, position size = 50 shares
   - Show how this means you can be wrong 10+ times in a row and still have 80%+ of your capital

4. **Position sizing as a probability-weighted decision**
   - Higher conviction (more confirming signals) → larger position (still within risk limits)
   - Lower conviction → smaller position or paper trade
   - Conviction hierarchy: 3+ signals = full size, 2 signals = half, 1 signal = quarter or skip

5. **Stop losses: the constraint that makes everything else work**
   - Types: fixed percentage, technical (below support), volatility-based (ATR multiplier)
   - When to use each. The honest truth: there is no "best" stop loss. The best one is the one you actually execute.
   - The psychological challenge: placing the stop means accepting you might be wrong before entering. Most traders skip this because it's uncomfortable.

6. **Risk/reward ratio: the minimum viable edge**
   - If your win rate is 50%, you need R:R > 1:1 to profit
   - If your win rate is 40%, you need R:R > 1.5:1
   - Table showing breakeven R:R at various win rates
   - The insight: you don't need to be right most of the time. You need your winners to be meaningfully larger than your losers.

7. **Correlation risk (the hidden killer)**
   - 5 tech stocks is not 5 diversified positions — it's 1 position with 5x the size
   - How to check: do your positions move together? If yes, you have concentration risk regardless of how many tickers you hold.

### Article 3: How to Read a Chart Without Getting Overwhelmed

**SEO targets**: "how to read stock charts", "candlestick charts for beginners", "chart reading basics"
**Word count**: ~3,000
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Lightweight Charts with auto-detected trendlines, candlestick pattern recognition

**Plan:**

1. **What a chart actually represents**
   - A chart is a compressed history of every transaction in a security. Each candle encodes four prices (OHLC) and participation (volume).
   - Analogy: a chart is to a stock what a medical history is to a patient. It doesn't tell you the diagnosis, but it constrains the possibilities.

2. **Candlesticks: the only chart type you need to start**
   - Anatomy: body (open-close range), wicks (high-low extremes)
   - Green/bullish vs red/bearish — what each communicates about the session
   - Long wicks = rejection of price. Small body = indecision. Large body = conviction.
   - The key: each candle tells a micro-story about the battle between buyers and sellers.

3. **Volume: the confirmation layer**
   - Price tells you what happened. Volume tells you how many people agreed it should happen.
   - High volume + price move = conviction (sustainable)
   - Low volume + price move = thin participation (fragile)
   - Volume divergence: price making new highs on declining volume = warning sign

4. **Support and resistance: what they are structurally**
   - Not magic lines. Areas where previous transactions created memory in the market.
   - Support: a price level where previous buyers exist (they bought here before, they may again)
   - Resistance: a price level where previous sellers exist
   - The honest caveat: support and resistance are descriptive, not predictive. They work until they don't. The question is always: has the regime changed?

5. **Trend identification: the 3-second test**
   - Higher highs + higher lows = uptrend
   - Lower highs + lower lows = downtrend
   - Neither pattern = range/consolidation
   - You should be able to identify the trend in 3 seconds. If you can't, it's probably not trending.

6. **The information overload trap**
   - Adding more indicators to a chart doesn't add more information. Most indicators are derived from the same inputs (price and volume).
   - RSI, MACD, Stochastic, CCI — all measure the same thing (momentum) in slightly different ways.
   - Start with: price, volume, one moving average (20-day SMA). Add complexity only when you can articulate what additional information each tool provides.

**Word count**: ~2,500
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Market sentiment analysis, news feed, institutional holdings

   - Only three forces move stock prices: supply/demand imbalance, new information, and sentiment shift.
   - Everything else (indicators, patterns, astrology) is a derivative of these three.

2. **Force 1: Supply and demand**
   - The observation: more buyers than sellers at current price → price rises. This is tautologically true.
   - The structural insight: what creates buying/selling pressure? Institutional rebalancing, index inclusion/exclusion, margin calls, fund flows.
   - The accessible truth: price is not "fair value." It's the intersection of urgency between buyers and sellers. Urgency changes; so does price.

3. **Force 2: New information**
   - Earnings, FDA decisions, macro data, geopolitical events
   - The efficient market hypothesis (presented honestly): in theory, prices instantly adjust to new information. In practice, they adjust quickly but imperfectly.

4. **Force 3: Sentiment**
   - The least tangible, most powerful short-term force
   - Sentiment is not a fact about the company. It's a fact about market participants' collective emotional state.
   - Fear and greed are not symmetric. Fear is faster and more intense than greed. This is why crashes are faster than rallies.
   - VIX as sentiment measurement (what it actually measures vs. how it's popularly interpreted)

5. **How the three interact**
   - Information triggers sentiment which drives supply/demand
   - But: sentiment can drive supply/demand without new information (momentum, herding)
   - And: supply/demand imbalances can occur mechanically without either (index rebalancing, options expiration)

### Article 5: Technical Analysis vs Fundamental Analysis: Which One Should You Use?

**SEO targets**: "technical vs fundamental analysis", "which analysis is better for trading"
**Word count**: ~3,000
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: AlphaBreak bridges both (the core value proposition)

**Plan:**

1. **The false dichotomy**
   - This is usually presented as a binary choice. It isn't. They answer different questions.
   - Fundamental: "What is this company worth?" (valuation problem)
   - Technical: "What are participants doing right now?" (behavior problem)
   - Analogy: asking "should I use a map or a compass?" You need both.

2. **What fundamental analysis can and cannot do**
   - Can: identify whether a stock is cheap or expensive relative to its earnings, assets, and growth
   - Cannot: tell you when the market will agree with your assessment. Being right about value is useless if the market takes 3 years to reprice.
   - The constraint: fundamental analysis requires patience and the capital to survive being "early" (which feels identical to being wrong)

3. **What technical analysis can and cannot do**
   - Can: identify when market participants are acting in ways that historically precede directional moves
   - Cannot: tell you why they're acting that way, or whether this time is different
   - The constraint: technical analysis requires regime awareness and acceptance that patterns are probabilistic, not deterministic

4. **The synthesis: use both, for different functions**
   - Fundamental analysis to select what to trade (filter universe to undervalued/overvalued)
   - Technical analysis to time when to trade (entry, exit, position sizing)
   - This isn't a novel insight — it's what most successful fund managers actually do. The debate exists in education, not in practice.

5. **Which to start with based on your style**
   - Day/swing traders: technical primary, fundamental secondary (time horizon too short for value to matter)
   - Position traders: both equally weighted
   - Investors (months-years): fundamental primary, technical for entry timing

### Article 6: Why Your Emotions Are Your Biggest Trading Enemy (And How to Control Them)

**SEO targets**: "trading psychology", "emotional trading", "FOMO trading"
**Word count**: ~3,500
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: AI trade scoring removes emotional bias, journal tracks emotional patterns

**Plan:**

1. **The emotional cycle of a trade**
   - Map the typical emotional arc: excitement (entry) → anxiety (drawdown) → relief/euphoria (win) or despair (loss) → revenge/FOMO (next trade)
   - This is not a character flaw. It's hardwired neurology. The amygdala processes loss as threat; the same brain region that activates when facing physical danger activates when facing financial loss.

2. **FOMO: the most expensive emotion**
   - Definition: entering a trade because you feel you're missing out, not because your system signals entry
   - Why it's structurally destructive: FOMO entries are late entries. By definition, you're buying after the move has happened. This means your risk/reward is worse than the people who entered on signal.
   - The constraint-based fix: if you missed the entry, the trade doesn't exist for your system. The next setup will come. Markets generate opportunities continuously.

3. **Revenge trading: the compounding error**
   - After a loss, the urge to "make it back" leads to larger position sizes and lower-quality setups
   - The math: if you lose 2% and then revenge trade at 4% risk with a worse setup, you've transformed a normal loss into a potential 6% drawdown
   - Rule: after any loss larger than planned, stop trading for the day. Non-negotiable.

4. **Loss aversion: holding losers, cutting winners**
   - Prospect theory (accessible version): losing $100 feels roughly 2x worse than gaining $100 feels good
   - This asymmetry causes traders to hold losers (hoping to avoid the pain of realizing the loss) and cut winners (locking in the pleasure of a gain)
   - The result: small wins, large losses. Negative expected value even with a positive hit rate.

5. **Practical emotion management systems**
   - Pre-trade checklist (forces deliberate thinking before emotional action)
   - Position size rules that are pre-committed (removes in-the-moment sizing decisions)
   - Trading journal with emotional state tracking (after 50 entries, patterns emerge)
   - Time-based rules: no trading in first 15 min of market open (highest emotional volatility)

### Article 7: The Illusion of Control: Why "Feeling It" Is Usually Wrong

**SEO targets**: "overconfidence in trading", "trading biases", "cognitive biases trading"
**Word count**: ~2,500
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: AI scoring provides objective assessment vs. gut feeling

**Plan:**

1. **The illusion defined**
   - Humans overestimate their ability to influence random or semi-random outcomes. This is experimentally verified across hundreds of studies.
   - In trading: the feeling of "I'm good at this" after a winning streak is statistically indistinguishable from the feeling a gambler has during a hot streak at craps.

2. **Distinguishing skill from luck (the fundamental problem)**
   - In high-skill activities (chess), outcomes correlate strongly with ability. Short sample sizes still reveal skill.
   - In high-luck activities (trading), outcomes have enormous variance. You need hundreds of trades before you can statistically separate skill from luck.
   - The honest framework: after 10 trades, you know nothing about your skill. After 100, you have a weak signal. After 500, you have meaningful data.
   - Formula (accessible): minimum sample size for statistical confidence in your edge

3. **The four cognitive biases that destroy traders**
   - Confirmation bias: seeking information that supports your position, ignoring contradictions
   - Recency bias: overweighting recent events (last 3 trades) vs. full history (last 300)
   - Anchoring: fixating on your entry price as if the market cares what you paid
   - Narrative bias: constructing stories to explain random price movement after the fact

4. **The antidote: systematic decision-making**
   - Write your thesis before entering (prevents post-hoc narrative)
   - Define invalidation before entering (prevents anchoring)
   - Review 50+ trade sample sizes, not individual trades (prevents recency)
   - Actively seek disconfirming evidence (prevents confirmation bias)

### Article 8: How to Handle Losing Streaks Without Quitting

**SEO targets**: "losing streak trading", "trading drawdown", "how to recover from trading losses"
**Word count**: ~2,500
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Portfolio equity curve, AI scoring identifies whether losses are systematic or variance

**Plan:**

1. **Losing streaks are mathematically inevitable**
   - Even a strategy with 60% win rate has a 6.5% chance of 5 consecutive losses
   - Table showing probability of N consecutive losses at various win rates
   - This is not failure. It's statistics. The question is: was the streak bad luck or a broken system?

2. **How to tell: variance vs. system failure**
   - Variance: losses are random, spread across different setups, and position sizes were correct
   - System failure: losses cluster in one setup type, or market regime has changed
   - The diagnostic: did you follow your rules? If yes, it's variance. If no, it's execution failure.

3. **The emotional progression of a drawdown**
   - Stage 1: "Normal variance" (confident)
   - Stage 2: "This is getting uncomfortable" (doubt)
   - Stage 3: "Maybe my system is broken" (fear)
   - Stage 4: "I need to change everything" (panic)
   - Stage 4 is where most traders abandon working systems. The irony: they quit right before the mean-reversion that would have made them whole.

4. **The protocol for surviving drawdowns**
   - Reduce position size by 50% (reduces financial and emotional pain)
   - Review last 20 trades against your system rules (diagnose, don't assume)
   - Paper trade for 1 week if doubt is overwhelming (removes financial risk while maintaining practice)
   - Set a maximum drawdown threshold in advance (e.g., 15%). If reached, stop live trading for 30 days.

5. **Reframing: the long-term perspective**
   - Every successful trader has experienced multiple 10-20% drawdowns
   - The difference: they had position sizing that prevented drawdowns from becoming fatal
   - Your job is not to avoid losses. It's to survive them.

### Article 10: The Power of Probabilities: Thinking in Edge Instead of "Right or Wrong"

**SEO targets**: "expected value trading", "probability trading", "trading edge"
**Word count**: ~3,000
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: AI trade scoring, quant grades, trend break probability percentages

**Plan:**

1. **The binary trap**
   - New traders think in binary: "Will this trade win or lose?"
   - Professional traders think in distributions: "Over 100 instances of this setup, what is my expected outcome?"
   - This shift — from prediction to probability — is the single most important cognitive upgrade in trading.

2. **Expected value explained (without math anxiety)**
   - EV = (win rate × average win) - (loss rate × average loss)
   - Example: 45% win rate, avg win $300, avg loss $150
     - EV = (0.45 × 300) - (0.55 × 150) = 135 - 82.50 = +$52.50 per trade
   - This strategy loses more often than it wins, but it makes money. This violates intuition, which is why most traders abandon profitable strategies.

3. **The casino analogy (precise version)**
   - A casino doesn't know whether the next hand of blackjack will win. They know their edge is 1-5% per hand.
   - Over 10 hands: anything can happen. Over 10,000 hands: the casino always wins.
   - Your trading strategy is the casino. Each trade is a hand. You need enough hands for the edge to manifest.

4. **How to measure your edge**
   - Minimum 50 trades before evaluating (less is noise)
   - Metrics: win rate, average win, average loss, profit factor, Sharpe ratio
   - The crucial question: is the edge large enough to survive transaction costs and emotional execution error?

5. **Edge decay and adaptation**
   - Edges erode over time as more participants exploit them
   - Review your metrics quarterly. If edge is declining, investigate whether the regime has changed or the signal has been crowded.

### Article 11: Support and Resistance: Why Price Respects These Levels (And When It Doesn't)

**SEO targets**: "support and resistance trading", "how to identify support levels", "support resistance strategy"
**Word count**: ~3,000
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Auto-detected trendlines, horizontal S/R levels from pivot clustering

**Plan:**

1. **What support and resistance actually are (structurally)**
   - Not magic lines. Memory points in the market.
   - Support: a concentration of previous buying activity. Participants who bought here before have a psychological anchor and may buy again.
   - Resistance: a concentration of previous selling activity. Participants who sold here may sell again.
   - The mechanism: anchoring bias across thousands of participants creates emergent price behavior.

2. **Why price "respects" these levels**
   - Self-fulfilling prophecy component: because many traders watch the same levels, they act on them, which reinforces them
   - Structural component: institutional orders often cluster at round numbers and previous pivot points
   - The honest caveat: S/R is descriptive, not causal. There is no physical law requiring price to stop at these levels.

3. **How to identify meaningful levels vs. noise**
   - Multiple touches (3+ touches = stronger level)
   - Multiple timeframes (daily and weekly agree = stronger)
   - Volume confirmation (high volume at the level = more participants anchored there)
   - Recency (recent levels matter more than old ones)

4. **When support and resistance fail**
   - Regime change: new information makes old price memory irrelevant
   - Volume breakout: high-volume move through a level indicates the composition of participants has changed
   - Gap through: opening significantly beyond a level (news event) leaves no "memory" to interact with

5. **Practical application**
   - Use S/R for stop placement (below support for longs, above resistance for shorts)
   - Use S/R for profit targets (take profit at next resistance level)
   - Never use S/R alone as entry signal — require additional confirmation

### Article 17: Why Most Backtests Lie (And How to Spot Fake Results)

**SEO targets**: "backtesting trading strategy", "overfitting trading", "backtest problems"
**Word count**: ~3,000
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: 854K trades backtested with honest methodology, transparent metrics

**Plan:**

1. **The backtest promise vs. reality**
   - Promise: "This strategy returned 200% over 5 years!"
   - Reality: most backtests overfit to historical data and fail in live trading
   - The honest truth: a backtest tells you how a strategy would have performed if you had known in advance which strategy to pick. This is circular.

2. **The five backtest sins**
   - **Overfitting**: Adding parameters until the strategy fits historical data perfectly. More parameters = more overfitting risk.
   - **Survivorship bias**: Testing only on stocks that still exist (ignoring delisted companies that went to zero)
   - **Look-ahead bias**: Using information that wasn't available at the time of the signal (e.g., adjusted close prices)
   - **Selection bias**: Running 100 strategies and publishing only the one that worked
   - **Transaction cost blindness**: Ignoring spread, slippage, and commissions (which can erase a small edge entirely)

3. **How to evaluate a backtest honestly**
   - Sample size: how many trades? (<100 is meaningless)
   - Out-of-sample testing: was the strategy developed on one period and tested on another?
   - Parameter sensitivity: does the strategy break if you change one input slightly? (If yes, it's overfit)
   - Regime diversity: did the test period include both bull and bear markets?
   - Transaction costs: were realistic costs included?

4. **The walk-forward approach (the gold standard)**
   - Develop strategy on Period A
   - Test on Period B (unseen data)
   - Then test on Period C (more unseen data)
   - Only if it works on B AND C is there evidence of a real edge

5. **The honest conclusion**
   - Backtesting is useful for disproving strategies (if it doesn't work in backtest, it probably won't work live)
   - It's unreliable for proving strategies (working in backtest doesn't guarantee live performance)
   - Use backtests as a filter, not a guarantee

### Article 18: Building a Watchlist That Actually Works

**SEO targets**: "trading watchlist", "how to build a stock watchlist", "stock screening"
**Word count**: ~2,500
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Watchlist management with batch fetch, real-time indicators, trend break alerts

**Plan:**

1. **The purpose of a watchlist (constraint function)**
   - A watchlist is not a list of stocks you like. It's a constraint that focuses your attention on opportunities with the highest probability of matching your setup.
   - Without a watchlist: you scan 5,000+ stocks and find nothing or everything. Decision paralysis.
   - With a curated watchlist: you scan 15-30 stocks and identify the 2-3 best setups.

2. **The filtering hierarchy**
   - Layer 1: Universe (large cap? small cap? specific sectors?)
   - Layer 2: Liquidity filter (minimum volume, minimum price — you need to be able to enter and exit)
   - Layer 3: Regime filter (is this stock trending, ranging, or in a favorable setup?)
   - Layer 4: Catalyst filter (upcoming earnings, sector rotation, news driver?)

3. **The rotation principle**
   - Watchlists should be dynamic, not static
   - Weekly review: remove stocks that no longer meet criteria, add new ones that do
   - Seasonal awareness: different sectors lead at different times

4. **Practical construction**
   - Start with a stock screener (filter by volume, price, sector)
   - Apply your specific setup criteria (e.g., RSI < 40 + above 200 SMA + in an uptrending sector)
   - Review charts of candidates, select 15-30 that are closest to your entry criteria
   - Rank by proximity to setup trigger

5. **The anti-watchlist: stocks to avoid**
   - Low volume (can't exit when you need to)
   - Binary events you don't understand (biotech FDA decisions, unless that's your specialty)
   - Stocks you're emotionally attached to (you'll override your system)

### Article 19: When to Cut a Losing Trade (The Psychology and Math)

**SEO targets**: "when to sell a losing stock", "stop loss trading", "cutting losses"
**Word count**: ~3,000
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Stop loss tracking in journal, AI annotations showing conditions at time of loss

**Plan:**

1. **The two types of losing trades**
   - Type 1: The trade was wrong (your thesis was invalidated — the setup failed)
   - Type 2: The trade is in a drawdown but the thesis is intact (normal variance)
   - These require opposite responses: Type 1 = exit immediately. Type 2 = hold (if within risk parameters).
   - The difficulty: distinguishing them in real-time, under emotional pressure.

2. **The math of cutting losses**
   - Revisit the asymmetry: -10% needs +11.1%, -50% needs +100%
   - The opportunity cost argument: capital stuck in a loser can't be deployed in a winner
   - Expected value of holding a losing trade vs. redeploying capital (usually negative for holding)

3. **The psychology of cutting losses**
   - Endowment effect: you value what you own more than what you don't (even a losing position)
   - Sunk cost fallacy: "I've already lost $500, I can't sell now" — but the $500 is gone regardless
   - Hope as strategy: "It'll come back" is not analysis. It's emotion disguised as patience.

4. **Rules-based exit framework**
   - Pre-define stop loss before entry (based on technical level, ATR, or fixed percentage)
   - The stop is the answer to: "At what price is my thesis invalidated?"
   - If price reaches the stop, exit. Period. No re-evaluation, no moving the stop.
   - Exception protocol: you may widen a stop only if new information supports the thesis AND you can articulate what changed

5. **The trailing stop approach**
   - Once a trade is profitable, move stop to breakeven (eliminates loss)
   - Then trail the stop below swing lows (locks in profit while allowing room)
   - This systematically creates asymmetric outcomes: capped losses, open-ended gains

### Article 20: From Beginner to Consistent: The 6-Month Roadmap

**SEO targets**: "learn trading in 6 months", "trading roadmap", "how to become a consistent trader"
**Word count**: ~4,000
**AlphaBreak tie-in**: Onboarding flow, learning path, all platform features as tools in the journey

   - The 6-month roadmap gets you to: "I understand my edge, I can manage risk, I have a system to follow"
   - Analogy: medical school is 4 years, residency is 3-7 more. Trading competence is not an overnight achievement.

2. **Month 1: Foundations**
   - Read articles 1-5 from this series
   - Open a paper trading account
   - Learn chart reading (candlesticks, volume, trend identification)
   - Study: 30 min/day reading, 30 min/day chart observation
   - Goal: be able to identify trend, support/resistance, and volume patterns by sight

   - Read articles 2 (risk management) and 9 (trading plan) deeply
   - Build your first trading plan (use the template)
   - Begin paper trading with strict 1% risk per trade
   - Journal every trade: entry reason, exit reason, emotional state, outcome
   - Goal: 20+ paper trades logged with full journal entries

4. **Month 3: Strategy selection and testing**
   - Read articles 10 (probabilities), 13 (trend vs. mean reversion)
   - Choose one strategy to focus on (don't split attention)
   - Backtest the strategy on 6 months of historical data
   - Continue paper trading with the selected strategy
   - Goal: 50+ trades, beginning to see patterns in journal

5. **Month 4: Live trading (small)**
   - Transition to live trading with minimum position sizes
   - The goal is not profit — it's execution. Can you follow your plan under real financial pressure?
   - Continue journaling. Add emotional state tracking.
   - Read articles 6-8 (psychology) — they'll hit differently now that you have live experience
   - Goal: 20+ live trades with plan adherence > 80%

   - Review all trades from months 3-4

   - Goal: written analysis of your first 100+ trades

   - Adjust strategy based on Month 5 analysis
   - Gradually increase position sizes (still within 1-2% risk)
   - Add one additional tool or confirmation (e.g., volume analysis, or institutional flow)
   - Read remaining articles as they become relevant to your specific needs
   - Goal: a refined trading plan, 150+ logged trades, clear understanding of your edge (or honest acknowledgment that you haven't found one yet)

8. **After Month 6: The fork in the road**
   - If metrics show positive expected value: continue scaling gradually
   - If metrics are flat: refine strategy, seek mentorship, give yourself 6 more months
   - If metrics are consistently negative: paper trade to diagnose whether it's the system or the execution
   - The honest truth: some people discover trading isn't for them. That's not failure — it's valuable self-knowledge that saves years and thousands of dollars.
